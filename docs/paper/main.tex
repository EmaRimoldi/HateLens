\begin{abstract}
We propose \method{}, a structured multi-task extension of parameter-efficient
fine-tuning (PEFT) of small decoder language models for hate speech detection.
Building on \citet{sen2024hatetinyllm}, who demonstrated that LoRA-tuned
TinyLlama-1.1B achieves competitive binary accuracy on DynaHate and HateEval,
we ask whether jointly predicting auxiliary attributes—hate type, target group,
and explicitness—together with optional rationale-token supervision and
pair-consistency regularization, is empirically associated with improved
out-of-distribution robustness.
We train on DynaHate (41k entries) with optional HateXplain joint data and
evaluate on three benchmarks: in-domain test splits, cross-benchmark transfer
to HateEval, and functional robustness via HateCheck (3,728 structured test cases).
Compared to a controlled binary baseline (\textsc{Bin-C}), our best structured
model (\textsc{Joint}) improves HateCheck F1 by $+1.6$ pp (0.905 → 0.921) and
DynaHate F1 by $+1.3$ pp (0.802 → 0.815), with only 780K trainable parameters.
However, a post-review head-path control (\textsc{Bin-FE}): a
\texttt{FEAT\_EXTR} binary model with no auxiliary losses—achieves HateCheck F1
of 0.937, showing that the $+1.6$ pp gain is primarily architectural rather than
a consequence of multi-task supervision.
A separate data-isolation control (\textsc{HX-no-rat}) shows that HateXplain
\emph{data}—not the rationale loss—is the primary driver of HateEval
cross-benchmark improvement (0.645 vs.\ Joint 0.615).
Multi-seed evaluation (three seeds) confirms that HateCheck gains are within
variance ($\pm 1.7$ pp for \textsc{Bin-C}), preventing strong causal claims.
A controlled PEFT comparison (LoRA, QLoRA, DoRA) on a binary setup shows all
three variants within 1 pp on HateCheck F1 (0.870–0.880), supporting the use of
quantized adapters for deployment.
Code and data loaders are publicly available.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/EmaRimoldi/HateLens}}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Automated hate speech detection is a critical component of online content
moderation, yet even strong models trained on benchmark datasets remain
brittle when transferred to new topics, targets, or phrasing styles.
A recurring finding across evaluations is that high held-out accuracy does not
guarantee robustness on the edge cases that matter most in deployment: denial
of hate (\textit{"I'm just joking"}), references involving reclaimed slurs, or
compositional rephrasing that preserves hateful intent while evading keyword
filters \citep{rottger2021hatecheck}.

\paragraph{The gap in existing work.}
\citet{sen2024hatetinyllm} showed that LoRA fine-tuning of small decoder
language models (TinyLlama, Phi-2, OPT-1.3B) achieves over 80\% accuracy on
DynaHate \citep{vidgen2021dynahate} and HateEval \citep{basile2019semeval}—
competitive with models ten times larger.
Their work focuses exclusively on binary classification: is a post hateful or not?
Binary supervision provides no signal about \emph{why} a post should be
classified as hateful, nor about the \emph{type} or \emph{target} of the hate.
Two practical problems follow from this: (1) models that latch onto surface
features—slur presence, identity terms—fail on adversarially generated edge
cases of the kind HateCheck probes; (2) binary models trained on a single dataset
transfer poorly to out-of-distribution benchmarks even when in-domain accuracy
is high.

\paragraph{Our approach.}
We extend TinyLlama+LoRA with \emph{structured multi-task supervision}: a shared
PEFT backbone is joined by lightweight heads that predict the primary hate label,
the hate type (\eg derogation, animosity, dehumanization), the target group
(\eg race, gender), and the explicitness of expression.
When HateXplain rationale annotations are available, we add token-level rationale
supervision \citep{mathew2021hatexplain}.
When DynaHate contrastive pair metadata is available, we apply pair-consistency
regularization.
The backbone remains frozen except for LoRA adapters on the key/value projections,
keeping trainable parameters at 780K.

Multi-task and multi-aspect hate detection has been studied before, largely with
encoder models and shared tasks (see Section~\ref{sec:related}).
Our contribution is narrower but practically motivated: we bring structured
supervision into the tiny-decoder PEFT regime, quantify the controlled effect
against a matched binary baseline, and evaluate robustness with HateCheck—a
combination that has not been systematically reported in this setup.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We report that structured multi-task supervision is associated with
    improved HateCheck robustness ($+1.6$ pp F1: 0.905 → 0.921, \textsc{Bin-C}
    vs.\ \textsc{Joint}) and DynaHate in-domain F1 ($+1.3$ pp) over a
    controlled binary baseline using identical PEFT configuration and the same
    training data.
    The larger $+10.6$ pp gain vs.\ the legacy baseline (\textsc{Bin-L}) is
    partly attributable to training-configuration differences rather than
    structured objectives alone.
  \item We present ablation studies consistent with pair-consistency
    regularization improving cross-benchmark transfer to HateEval ($+2.1$ pp
    over \textsc{Struct}), and joint HateXplain training adding $+0.8$ pp
    HateCheck F1.
    Because controls are partially confounded (Section~\ref{sec:limits}),
    we report these as empirical associations, not causal claims.
  \item We compare LoRA, QLoRA, and DoRA under controlled binary conditions,
    finding all three within 1 pp on HateCheck F1 and validating quantized
    adapters for deployment.
  \item We release \method{}, an open codebase with reproducible pipelines,
    unified evaluation including calibration metrics (ECE, Brier score),
    and all data loaders.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Hate speech detection with PLMs.}
BERT-based encoders established strong baselines for automated hate detection
\citep{basile2019semeval,vidgen2021dynahate}.
\citet{rottger2021hatecheck} introduced HateCheck to expose systematic weaknesses
in these models—including over-sensitivity to identity terms and failure on
negated hate—finding critical errors in all tested systems.
More recent work shifted toward encoder-only fine-tuning with dataset
unification: \citet{elbahnasawi2025lora} combine LoRA-tuned BERTweet with
rule-based pre-filtering and continuous learning, achieving macro F1 0.85
with only 1.87M trainable parameters on unified datasets.

\paragraph{Tiny decoder LLMs for classification.}
\citet{sen2024hatetinyllm} proposed HateTinyLLM, fine-tuning TinyLlama (1.1B),
Phi-2 (2.7B), and OPT-1.3B with LoRA and adapters on DynaHate and HateEval,
observing that all LoRA-tuned models exceed 80\% accuracy—outperforming a 7B
pretrained Mixtral baseline.
Our work directly extends this line by replacing the binary head with a
structured multi-head classifier while preserving the PEFT setup.

\paragraph{Multi-task and multi-aspect hate detection.}
Multi-task supervision for hate speech is established prior work.
\citet{vidgen2021dynahate} already provide fine-grained type and target annotations
in DynaHate; joint hate+target learning has been explored since \citep{basile2019semeval}.
\citet{mathew2021hatexplain} introduced HateXplain specifically to support
rationale-annotated and target-annotated supervision.
Multi-task learning over hate type, target, and severity has been explored in
recent shared tasks \citep{prama2025hatesense}.
Contrastive and pair-based training for cross-dataset generalization also has
precedent in the NLI literature \citep{gardner2020contrast}.
The contribution of this paper is not a new multi-task paradigm; rather, it is
an empirical study of whether structured supervision helps robustness in the
specific setting of tiny decoder LMs trained with LoRA-family PEFT, evaluated
on HateCheck and cross-benchmark transfer—a combination that has not been
systematically quantified.

\paragraph{PEFT variants.}
LoRA \citep{hu2022lora} has become the de facto PEFT method for classification.
QLoRA \citep{dettmers2023qlora} extends this with 4-bit quantization for memory
efficiency.
DoRA \citep{liu2024dora} decomposes weight updates into magnitude and direction
components.
Their relative performance on hate detection under controlled conditions has not
been directly compared; we provide such a comparison in a binary classification
setting.

\paragraph{Calibration and transfer in hate detection.}
Model calibration—the alignment between predicted confidence and true accuracy—
is rarely reported in hate detection benchmarks despite its relevance for
deployment \citep{guo2017calibration}.
We report Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and Brier score alongside
standard classification metrics for all models.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Backbone and PEFT}
We use \tl (1.1B parameters, \texttt{PY007/TinyLlama-1.1B-step-50K-105b}) as
the backbone decoder LM.
For binary classification experiments we attach a sequence-classification
head via \texttt{AutoModelForSequenceClassification}
(\texttt{task\_type=SEQ\_CLS}).
For structured experiments we use \texttt{task\_type=FEATURE\_EXTRACTION} and
replace the classification head with the multi-head architecture described below.

\emph{Note on head path.}
Binary and structured models differ in both the head architecture and the
internal PEFT task type (\texttt{SEQ\_CLS} vs.\ \texttt{FEATURE\_EXTRACTION}).
This is a confound between the binary and structured model comparisons:
any observed differences may reflect the changed computation path, not only
the auxiliary objectives.
A single-head, same-path control is identified as a priority for future work.

LoRA adapters are applied to the key ($W_K$) and value ($W_V$) projections in
all self-attention layers with rank $r=8$, scaling factor $\alpha=16$, and
dropout $p=0.05$.
This yields 780,669 trainable parameters (0.075\% of 1.04B total).

\subsection{Structured Multi-Head Classifier}
\label{sec:structured}

The structured model appends four lightweight linear heads to the last-layer
[EOS] token representation:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Main head} ($h_\text{main}$): binary hate vs.\ not-hate,
    trained with cross-entropy.
  \item \textbf{Hate-type head} ($h_\text{type}$): 5-way classification
    (Animosity, Derogation, Dehumanization, Threatening, Support for Hateful
    Entities) from DynaHate type annotations, active on hateful samples only,
    trained with cross-entropy.
  \item \textbf{Target head} ($h_\text{target}$): multi-label prediction over
    target groups (race, gender, religion, sexual orientation, disability,
    other) derived from DynaHate target annotations, trained with
    \emph{binary cross-entropy per label} (BCE-with-logits).
  \item \textbf{Explicitness head} ($h_\text{exp}$): binary explicit
    vs.\ implicit, derived from DynaHate by binarising the type annotation
    (Dehumanization / Threatening mapped to explicit; Animosity / Derogation /
    Support treated as implicit); active on hateful samples only,
    trained with cross-entropy.
    This binarisation is an approximation; explicit/implicit labels from an
    independent annotation source would be preferable.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The combined loss is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\text{main}
               + \lambda_\text{aux}\,\mathcal{L}_\text{aux}
               + \lambda_r\,\mathcal{L}_\text{rationale}
               + \lambda_c\,\mathcal{L}_\text{consist},
  \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_\text{aux}$ is the sum of the type, target, and explicitness
head losses (gated on hateful examples);
$\mathcal{L}_\text{rationale}$ is token-level BCE over HateXplain rationale
spans (gated to zero when no span annotation is available for a sample);
$\mathcal{L}_\text{consist}$ is the pair-consistency margin loss (defined in
Section~\ref{sec:consistency}, gated on contrastive pair availability).
Loss weights are $\lambda_\text{aux}=0.5$, $\lambda_r=0.3$, $\lambda_c=0.2$.

\subsection{Rationale Supervision}
\label{sec:rationale}

HateXplain provides annotator-marked token spans that justify a hate label
\citep{mathew2021hatexplain}.
We align these spans to the tokenizer's subword vocabulary and compute
token-level BCE over a dedicated rationale head output.
When HateXplain data is used in joint training with DynaHate, DynaHate
examples have no span annotations and receive zero rationale loss (the mask
gates the term to zero).
The \textsc{+Cons} ablation (DynaHate only, with consistency enabled) also has
$\lambda_r > 0$ in the config, but since no DynaHate sample carries span
annotations, the rationale term evaluates to zero throughout training.
The consistency-only effect is therefore cleanly isolated in \textsc{+Cons},
but the rationale term is not independently ablated from the data-mixing
effect in \textsc{Joint} (see Section~\ref{sec:limits}).

\subsection{Pair-Consistency Regularization}
\label{sec:consistency}

DynaHate records contrastive pairs: minimally different examples where the
intended label flips.
For each available pair $(x_i, x_j)$ with gold labels $y_i \neq y_j$, we
apply a margin loss:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{consist}(x_i, x_j)
    = \max\!\bigl(0,\; m - (p_{y_i}(x_i) - p_{y_i}(x_j))\bigr),
\end{equation}
with margin $m=0.1$, where $p_{y}(x)$ is the model's predicted probability
for the gold label $y$.
The loss pushes the model to assign higher probability to the correct label by
at least $m$ relative to the counterfactual example.
Non-hateful halves of pairs are treated as hard negatives.
This formulation incentivises consistent predictions across contrastive
perturbations but does not guarantee logical entailment; the mechanism
may also function as hard-negative mining rather than as consistency
enforcement per se.

\subsection{Training Details}
All experiments use AdamW \citep{loshchilov2019adamw} with learning rate
$2 \times 10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.01, effective batch size 8 (per-device 2,
gradient accumulation 4), mixed-precision FP16, and a linear warmup of 10
steps.
Binary experiments train for 1 epoch; structured experiments for 2 epochs.
The best checkpoint is selected by macro-F1 on the development set.
\emph{All experiments use a single random seed (123); multi-seed variance
is not reported and the effect of seed on the smaller observed deltas is
unknown.}
Training runs on a single NVIDIA A100-80GB GPU.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets}

\textbf{DynaHate} \citep{vidgen2021dynahate} contains 41,144 adversarially
generated English posts (54\% hate) with fine-grained type (5 categories) and
target annotations created over four rounds of human-and-model-in-the-loop
collection.
We use the official train/dev/test splits (v0.2.3).

\textbf{HateEval} \citep{basile2019semeval} is the SemEval-2019 Task 5 dataset
targeting hate against women and immigrants on Twitter ($\sim$9,000 English
entries).
We use the official training and development splits; the test labels released
post-competition serve as our HateEval test set.

\textbf{HateXplain} \citep{mathew2021hatexplain} provides 9,055 posts annotated
with hate/offensive/normal labels and token-level rationale spans.
We map hate and offensive to \texttt{hate} and use the dataset for joint
structured training only.
This remapping changes the effective positive class relative to datasets that
use a pure hate definition; any performance change from joint training could
reflect additional data, altered label semantics, or rationale supervision.

\textbf{HateCheck} \citep{rottger2021hatecheck} is a suite of 3,728 functional
tests across 29 functionalities.
We use it exclusively as a zero-shot robustness probe—no HateCheck examples
appear in training.

\subsection{Evaluation}
We report binary F1 (hate class), macro-F1, accuracy, AUC-ROC, AUC-PR,
Expected Calibration Error (ECE), and Brier score.
HateCheck results use overall binary F1 across all 3,728 cases.
All evaluation is performed with the \texttt{hatelens eval-run} CLI,
which writes structured \texttt{metrics.json} bundles covering in-domain,
cross-dataset, and HateCheck splits in a single pass.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Main Comparison: Binary vs.\ Structured}

Table~\ref{tab:main} presents the main results.
\textbf{Understanding the two binary baselines.}
\textsc{Bin-L} is a re-implementation of \citet{sen2024hatetinyllm} and
serves as a reference point; it achieves DynaHate F1 0.710 and HateCheck F1
0.815.
However, \textsc{Bin-L} trained for only 22 seconds (Table~\ref{tab:efficiency}),
suggesting a configuration artefact rather than a full training run.
\textsc{Bin-C} provides a fair comparison for the structured models: it uses
the same hyperparameter schedule (2 epochs, same batch size and LR) but with
the binary \texttt{SEQ\_CLS} head.
\textsc{Bin-C} raises DynaHate F1 to 0.802 and HateCheck F1 to 0.905, a large
jump that is attributable to training configuration alone.

\textbf{Structured vs.\ controlled binary.}
The structured model (\textsc{Struct}) raises DynaHate F1 by $+1.0$ pp and
HateCheck F1 by $+0.8$ pp over \textsc{Bin-C}, with no improvement on HateEval
cross-benchmark transfer (both 0.609).
The joint model (\textsc{Joint}) achieves HateCheck F1 of 0.921,
a $+1.6$ pp gain over \textsc{Bin-C} and $+10.6$ pp over the legacy
\textsc{Bin-L}.
Readers should interpret $+10.6$ pp with caution: most of this gap is explained
by the configuration difference between \textsc{Bin-L} and \textsc{Bin-C}, not
by the structured objectives.

\textbf{Head-path control (\textsc{Bin-FE}).}
To isolate the \texttt{FEAT\_EXTR} architecture from the auxiliary objectives,
we trained a binary model using the \texttt{FEAT\_EXTR} head with all auxiliary
losses disabled ($\lambda_\text{aux}=0$).
\textsc{Bin-FE} achieves HateCheck F1 of \textbf{0.937}—the highest across all
models—despite \emph{no} multi-task supervision.
This reveals that the head-path switch (\texttt{SEQ\_CLS}$\to$\texttt{FEAT\_EXTR})
alone contributes $+3.2$ pp HateCheck F1, while structured objectives on top
of \texttt{FEAT\_EXTR} yield no additional HateCheck benefit
(\textsc{Struct}: 0.913; \textsc{Joint}: 0.921; vs.\ \textsc{Bin-FE}: 0.937).
The $+1.6$ pp reported for \textsc{Joint} vs.\ \textsc{Bin-C} therefore
largely reflects the architectural difference rather than multi-task supervision.

\textbf{Data vs.\ rationale control (\textsc{HX-no-rat}).}
\textsc{HX-no-rat} loads HateXplain training data but sets $\lambda_\text{rat}=0$,
keeping consistency supervision while disabling token-level rationale loss.
It achieves HateEval cross-benchmark F1 of \textbf{0.645}, surpassing
\textsc{+Cons} ($+1.5$ pp) and \textsc{Joint} ($+3.0$ pp), with near-identical
HateCheck F1 (0.922 vs.\ Joint 0.921).
This indicates that the HateXplain \emph{data distribution}—not the rationale
supervision loss—is the primary driver of cross-domain transfer improvement.

\paragraph{Cross-benchmark transfer.}
Training on DynaHate and evaluating on HateEval is a domain-shift challenge.
Structured objectives alone (\textsc{Struct}) do not improve over \textsc{Bin-C}
(both 0.609).
Adding pair-consistency (\textsc{+Cons}) provides the largest observed gain to
0.630 ($+2.1$ pp over \textsc{Struct}).

\subsection{Ablation Study}

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} visualises the incremental contribution of each
objective.
Relative to \textsc{Bin-C}, the structured head alone (\textsc{Struct})
contributes $+0.8$ pp HateCheck F1 and $+1.0$ pp DynaHate F1.
Pair-consistency regularization (\textsc{+Cons}) contributes $+0.0$ pp on
HateCheck but $+2.1$ pp on HateEval cross-benchmark transfer, suggesting
that it is primarily associated with generalisation rather than in-domain
robustness.
Joint HateXplain training (\textsc{Joint}) adds a further $+0.8$ pp on
HateCheck F1.

\textbf{Multi-seed variance.}
To assess result stability, we re-trained \textsc{Bin-C} and \textsc{Struct}
with two additional seeds (42 and 456).
HateCheck F1 for \textsc{Bin-C} across three seeds: $0.905$, $0.938$, $0.918$
(mean $92.0 \pm 1.7$ pp).
For \textsc{Struct}: $0.913$, $0.924$, $0.916$ (mean $91.8 \pm 0.6$ pp).
The $+0.8$ pp structured vs.\ binary advantage (0.921 vs.\ 0.905) falls within
the Bin-C seed variance ($\pm 1.7$ pp), suggesting it is not reliably
reproducible.
DynaHate F1 is more stable: \textsc{Bin-C} $80.9 \pm 0.7$ pp;
\textsc{Struct} $81.4 \pm 0.2$ pp.

\textbf{Interpretation caveats.}
The controls reported above (\textsc{Bin-FE}, \textsc{HX-no-rat}) and the
multi-seed results substantially update the paper's original claims.
The head-path confound is the dominant driver of HateCheck gains;
HateXplain data (not rationale loss) drives HateEval transfer.
Section~\ref{sec:limits} elaborates on remaining gaps.

\subsection{PEFT Variant Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:peft} compares LoRA, QLoRA, and DoRA under matched conditions:

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why are structured objectives associated with improved HateCheck?}
HateCheck's 29 functional tests probe specific model behaviours—negation, slur
usage, counter-speech—that require compositional understanding rather than
surface-level pattern matching.
One plausible account is that predicting hate type and target during training
encourages the model to represent \emph{who} is targeted and \emph{how},
reducing reliance on identity-term correlation as a proxy for hate.
This is consistent with findings in \citet{mathew2021hatexplain}, where
rationale-supervised models showed better calibration and lower shortcut
susceptibility.
However, the head-path control (\textsc{Bin-FE}) demonstrated that the
\texttt{FEAT\_EXTR} computation path alone—without any auxiliary supervision—
achieves the highest HateCheck F1 (0.937), directly attributing the HateCheck
gain to architecture rather than multi-task objectives.
We therefore cannot claim that structured supervision improves HateCheck
robustness in any causally meaningful sense.

\paragraph{Pair-consistency and cross-benchmark transfer.}
The \textsc{+Cons} ablation shows the most improvement on HateEval cross-benchmark
transfer ($+2.1$ pp over \textsc{Struct}) with no HateCheck gain.
This pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that contrastive pair training
encourages representations that generalise across domain shifts.
An equally plausible explanation is that the margin loss functions as hard-negative
mining, improving recall on ambiguous examples.
Without a random-pair control or a same-data equivalent, these alternatives
cannot be distinguished.

\paragraph{PEFT variant choice.}
LoRA, QLoRA, and DoRA perform within 1 pp on HateCheck F1 in the binary
1-epoch setup.
Given memory savings at no accuracy cost, QLoRA is a pragmatic default for
inference on constrained hardware.
Whether this ranking transfers to the structured model requires further testing.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\label{sec:limits}
We identify the following gaps that prevent strong mechanistic conclusions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Single seed.} \emph{(Partially addressed.)}
    Section~\ref{sec:results} now reports three-seed evaluation for
    \textsc{Bin-C} and \textsc{Struct}.
    HateCheck variance is $\pm 1.7$ pp for \textsc{Bin-C} and $\pm 0.6$ pp
    for \textsc{Struct}; the $+1.6$ pp \textsc{Joint} vs.\ \textsc{Bin-C} delta
    falls within this range.
    All other models remain single-seed.

  \item \textbf{Head-path confound.} \emph{(Addressed.)}
    \textsc{Bin-FE} (Table~\ref{tab:main}) is the same-architecture control:
    \texttt{FEAT\_EXTR} head, binary CE only ($\lambda_\text{aux}=0$).
    It achieves HateCheck 0.937, higher than all structured models, confirming
    that the architecture switch—not multi-task supervision—is the primary
    driver of HateCheck gains.

  \item \textbf{Data and rationale confound.} \emph{(Addressed.)}
    \textsc{HX-no-rat} (Table~\ref{tab:main}) loads HateXplain data with
    $\lambda_\text{rat}=0$.
    It achieves the best HateEval F1 (0.645), confirming that data distribution
    shift, not rationale supervision, drives cross-domain transfer.
    The remaining unmeasured factor is the offensive→hate label remapping in
    HateXplain.

  \item \textbf{Consistency vs.\ hard-negative mining.}
    The pair-consistency loss may help because it provides informative negatives
    or a ranking signal, not because it enforces logical invariance.
    A random-pair or same-class contrastive control would distinguish these
    explanations.

  \item \textbf{Explicitness label quality.}
    The explicitness head uses a type-binarisation approximation.
    Explicit/implicit labels from an independent annotation study would
    strengthen the auxiliary supervision.

  \item \textbf{English and TinyLlama only.}
    Generalisability to other languages or larger decoder models is untested.

  \item \textbf{Trainable-parameter reporting.}
    The evaluation pipeline did not capture trainable parameter counts for binary
    runs; Table~\ref{tab:efficiency} shows \emph{(not cap.)} for those rows.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented an empirical study of structured multi-task supervision for hate
speech detection with tiny decoder LMs under LoRA-family PEFT.
Post-review control experiments substantially revise the original interpretation:
a head-path control (\textsc{Bin-FE}) shows that switching from \texttt{SEQ\_CLS}
to \texttt{FEAT\_EXTR} alone—without any multi-task objectives—yields the highest
HateCheck F1 (0.937), exceeding all structured models.
The $+1.6$ pp gain reported for \textsc{Joint} vs.\ the controlled binary
baseline (\textsc{Bin-C}) is therefore primarily attributable to the
architectural head-path difference, not to multi-task supervision.
A data-isolation control (\textsc{HX-no-rat}) further shows that HateXplain
data inclusion—not the rationale loss—drives the best cross-domain transfer
(HateEval F1 0.645).
Multi-seed evaluation confirms that observed HateCheck deltas ($\pm 1.7$ pp
for \textsc{Bin-C}) are within single-seed variance.
Taken together, these findings suggest that architectural and data choices
dominate over the specific form of multi-task supervision in this regime.
A controlled PEFT comparison confirms LoRA, QLoRA, and DoRA are interchangeable,
supporting quantized adapters for deployment.
We release the \method{} codebase with a unified evaluation CLI, calibration
metrics, and all data loaders to support reproducibility and follow-up research.
